UNIT I — NUMBER SYSTEMS (6 Marks)
Ch 1
Real Numbers
Low Weightage 6 Marks (Unit I)
Topic Breakdown
  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
    • Statement of the theorem (every composite number expressible as product of primes)
      • Unique prime factorisation of natural numbers
      • Motivating through examples (e.g., factorising 156, 3825)
      • Illustrations connecting to earlier knowledge (HCF and LCM)
    • Applying to find HCF and LCM using prime factorisation
      • HCF = product of smallest powers of common prime factors
      • LCM = product of greatest powers of all prime factors
      • Relationship: HCF × LCM = Product of two numbers
  • Irrationality Proofs
    • Proof that √2 is irrational
      • Proof by contradiction method
      • Assumption: √2 = p/q where p, q are co-prime integers
      • Deriving contradiction using divisibility
    • Proof that √3 is irrational (same technique as √2)
    • Proof that √5 is irrational (same technique)
    • Proving composite forms like 3 + 2√5 is irrational
      • Using contradiction: assuming rational, isolating irrational part
      • Showing rational = irrational leads to contradiction
Competency Focus Understanding properties of real numbers; extending power/exponent understanding; applying FTA in real-life contexts.
UNIT II — ALGEBRA (20 Marks — Highest Weightage)
Ch 2
Polynomials
High Weightage Unit Part of 20 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Zeros of a Polynomial
    • Geometrical meaning of zeros — where graph crosses the x-axis
    • Finding zeros graphically (reading x-intercepts)
      • Parabola graphs for quadratic polynomials
      • Zero, one, or two intersections with x-axis
    • Finding zeros algebraically (by factorisation, middle-term splitting)
  • Relationship Between Zeros and Coefficients
    • For quadratic polynomial ax² + bx + c:
      • Sum of zeros = −b/a
      • Product of zeros = c/a
      • Forming quadratic polynomial when zeros are given: x² − (sum)x + product
      • Verifying relationship with calculated zeros
Ch 3
Pair of Linear Equations in Two Variables
High Weightage Unit Part of 20 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Graphical Method of Solution
    • Plotting two linear equations on the same graph
      • Intersection point = unique solution (consistent)
      • Parallel lines = no solution (inconsistent)
      • Coincident lines = infinitely many solutions (dependent)
  • Algebraic Conditions for Number of Solutions
    • Unique solution: a₁/a₂ ≠ b₁/b₂
    • No solution: a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ ≠ c₁/c₂
    • Infinitely many solutions: a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ = c₁/c₂
  • Algebraic Methods
    • Substitution method
      • Express one variable in terms of the other; substitute and solve
    • Elimination method
      • Multiply equations to equalise coefficients; add or subtract to eliminate one variable
    • Simple situational/word problems (age problems, number problems, geometry-based)
Ch 4
Quadratic Equations
High Weightage Unit Part of 20 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Standard Form
    • ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0
    • Identifying and converting equations to standard form
  • Methods of Solving Quadratic Equations
    • Factorisation method
      • Splitting the middle term
      • Setting each factor to zero
    • Quadratic formula (Shridharacharya's formula)
      • x = [−b ± √(b² − 4ac)] / 2a
      • Only real roots are in scope
  • Discriminant and Nature of Roots
    • Discriminant D = b² − 4ac
      • D > 0 → two distinct real roots
      • D = 0 → two equal real roots (coincident)
      • D < 0 → no real roots
  • Real-life Word Problems
    • Problems involving areas, speeds, time-and-distance, upstream/downstream, consecutive numbers
    • Formulating quadratic equations from word problems and solving
Ch 5
Arithmetic Progressions (AP)
High Weightage Unit Part of 20 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Introduction and Motivation
    • Definition: sequence where difference between consecutive terms is constant
    • Common difference d = aₙ − aₙ₋₁ (constant)
    • First term a, common difference d as defining parameters
  • nth Term Formula
    • aₙ = a + (n−1)d
      • Finding nth term given a and d
      • Finding n (which term) given aₙ
      • Finding a or d from given information
  • Sum of First n Terms
    • Sₙ = n/2 × [2a + (n−1)d]
    • Sₙ = n/2 × (a + l), where l = last term
    • Derivation of the formula (Gauss's method)
  • Applications
    • Real-life problems: savings, stacking objects, arranging seats, salary increments
    • Finding number of terms between two terms
    • Problems where aₙ is given and finding Sₙ or vice versa
UNIT III — COORDINATE GEOMETRY (6 Marks)
Ch 6
Coordinate Geometry
Low Weightage 6 Marks (Unit III)
Topic Breakdown
  • Review of Coordinate Geometry Concepts
    • Cartesian plane, axes, origin, quadrants
    • Plotting points in all four quadrants
  • Distance Formula
    • Distance between two points A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂)
      • AB = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
      • Distance of a point from origin: √(x² + y²)
    • Applications: proving collinearity, type of quadrilateral (square, rhombus, rectangle, parallelogram)
  • Section Formula (Internal Division only)
    • Point dividing line segment in ratio m:n internally
      • x = (mx₂ + nx₁)/(m+n), y = (my₂ + ny₁)/(m+n)
      • Midpoint formula as special case (m=n=1): ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
      • Finding ratio of division given the dividing point
Important Note External division is NOT in the syllabus for 2026–27. Only internal division is prescribed.
UNIT IV — GEOMETRY (15 Marks)
Ch 7
Triangles
High Weightage Unit Part of 15 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Similar Triangles — Definitions and Examples
    • Definition of similarity: same shape, not necessarily same size
    • Difference between congruence (≅) and similarity (~)
    • Counter-examples to wrong assumptions about similarity
  • Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT / Thales' Theorem) — PROOF REQUIRED
    • Statement: If a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle to intersect the other two sides in distinct points, it divides those sides in the same ratio
    • Proof using area method (construction of equal triangles)
    • Converse of BPT — State without proof
  • Similarity Criteria — State without Proof
    • AA (Angle-Angle) similarity criterion
    • SSS (Side-Side-Side) similarity criterion
    • SAS (Side-Angle-Side) similarity criterion
  • Applications of Similar Triangles
    • Finding unknown sides in similar triangles
    • Proving triangles similar using given conditions
    • Combined problems using BPT and similarity criteria
Syllabus Note — 2026-27 Area of similar triangles and Pythagoras theorem (and its converse) have been REMOVED from the 2026–27 syllabus compared to older curricula. Only BPT (proof) and similarity criteria (statement only) are retained.
Ch 8
Circles
High Weightage Unit Part of 15 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Tangent to a Circle — Concepts
    • Definition of tangent at a point of contact
    • Point of contact, secant vs tangent distinction
  • Theorem 1 — PROOF REQUIRED
    • Statement: The tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact (OT ⊥ PT)
    • Proof using contradiction method
    • Applications: finding angles in figures with circles and tangents
  • Theorem 2 — PROOF REQUIRED
    • Statement: The lengths of tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal (PA = PB)
    • Proof using RHS congruence (OAP ≅ OBP)
    • Applications: problems involving tangent lengths from external points, perimeter of triangles circumscribing circles
Syllabus Note — 2026-27 Construction chapter has been completely REMOVED. Circles chapter is now focused only on the two tangent theorems (both proofs required) and their applications.
UNIT V — TRIGONOMETRY (12 Marks)
Ch 9
Introduction to Trigonometry
High Weightage Unit Part of 12 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Trigonometric Ratios
    • Six ratios of acute angles of a right-angled triangle
      • sin A = Opposite/Hypotenuse, cos A = Adjacent/Hypotenuse
      • tan A = Opposite/Adjacent = sin A / cos A
      • cosec A = 1/sin A, sec A = 1/cos A, cot A = 1/tan A
      • Proof of well-definedness (ratios are independent of which right triangle is used)
  • Trigonometric Ratios of Standard Angles
    • Values at 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°
      • sin 0°=0, sin 30°=1/2, sin 45°=1/√2, sin 60°=√3/2, sin 90°=1
      • cos: reverse pattern (1, √3/2, 1/√2, 1/2, 0)
      • tan: 0, 1/√3, 1, √3, undefined
      • Motivating ratios at 0° and 90° via limit thinking
  • Relationships Between Trigonometric Ratios
    • Reciprocal relations: cosec = 1/sin, sec = 1/cos, cot = 1/tan
    • Quotient relations: tan = sin/cos, cot = cos/sin
Ch 10
Trigonometric Identities
High Weightage Unit Part of 12 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Pythagorean Identities — PROOF REQUIRED
    • sin²A + cos²A = 1 — primary identity (proof using Pythagoras theorem)
    • Derived: 1 + tan²A = sec²A
    • Derived: 1 + cot²A = cosec²A
  • Application of Identities
    • Proving trigonometric identities (LHS = RHS)
      • Converting everything to sin and cos
      • Using algebraic manipulation (factorisation, rationalisation)
      • Only simple identities as per syllabus
Ch 11
Heights and Distances
High Weightage Unit Part of 12 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Angle of Elevation and Depression
    • Angle of elevation: angle above horizontal line to look UP at an object
    • Angle of depression: angle below horizontal line to look DOWN at an object
    • Drawing diagrams correctly for given scenarios
  • Solving Problems
    • Problems involving maximum two right triangles
    • Angles restricted to 30°, 45°, and 60° only
    • Setting up the correct ratio (tan, sin, cos) from the diagram
      • Finding height of buildings, towers, hills
      • Finding distance between two objects
      • Problems from two positions of observer
UNIT VI — MENSURATION (10 Marks)
Ch 12
Areas Related to Circles
Medium Weightage Unit Part of 10 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Area of Sector
    • Area of sector = (θ/360°) × πr²
    • Length of arc = (θ/360°) × 2πr
    • Perimeter of sector = 2r + arc length
  • Area of Segment
    • Minor segment = Area of sector − Area of triangle
    • Major segment = Area of circle − Area of minor segment
    • Problems restricted to central angles of 60°, 90°, and 120° only
  • Combined Figure Problems
    • Shaded/unshaded region problems combining circles with triangles, rectangles, squares
    • Problems on perimeters and areas of combined plane figures
Ch 13
Surface Areas and Volumes
Medium Weightage Unit Part of 10 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Surface Areas of Combinations of Solids
    • Cube: TSA = 6a², LSA = 4a²
    • Cuboid: TSA = 2(lb+bh+lh), LSA = 2(l+b)h
    • Cylinder: CSA = 2πrh, TSA = 2πr(r+h)
    • Cone: CSA = πrl, TSA = πr(r+l), l = √(r²+h²)
    • Sphere: TSA = 4πr²; Hemisphere: CSA = 2πr², TSA = 3πr²
    • Surface area of combinations (cone on cylinder, hemisphere on cylinder, etc.)
      • Identifying which faces are exposed and which are hidden at joints
  • Volumes of Combinations of Solids
    • Volume formulas: Cube (a³), Cuboid (lbh), Cylinder (πr²h), Cone (1/3 πr²h), Sphere (4/3 πr³), Hemisphere (2/3 πr³)
    • Combinations of any two of: cubes, cuboids, spheres, hemispheres, cylinders, cones
    • Conversion/melting/recasting problems (volume before = volume after)
UNIT VII — STATISTICS AND PROBABILITY (11 Marks)
Ch 14
Statistics
High Weightage Unit Part of 11 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Mean of Grouped Data
    • Direct Method: x̄ = Σfᵢxᵢ / Σfᵢ
    • Assumed Mean Method: x̄ = a + (Σfᵢdᵢ / Σfᵢ), where dᵢ = xᵢ − a
    • Step Deviation Method: x̄ = a + (Σfᵢuᵢ / Σfᵢ) × h, where uᵢ = (xᵢ−a)/h
    • Choosing appropriate method based on data complexity
  • Median of Grouped Data
    • Cumulative frequency table construction
    • Median = l + [(n/2 − cf)/f] × h
      • l = lower boundary of median class
      • cf = cumulative frequency before median class
      • f = frequency of median class
      • h = class width
  • Mode of Grouped Data
    • Modal class: class with highest frequency
    • Mode = l + [(f₁−f₀)/(2f₁−f₀−f₂)] × h
    • Bimodal situation to be AVOIDED (as per syllabus)
Ch 15
Probability
High Weightage Unit Part of 11 Marks
Topic Breakdown
  • Classical Definition of Probability
    • P(E) = Number of favourable outcomes / Total number of outcomes
    • Conditions: equally likely, mutually exclusive, exhaustive outcomes
    • Range of probability: 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1
    • P(Ē) = 1 − P(E) (complementary events)
  • Simple Probability Problems
    • Coins: single, double, triple coin experiments
      • Sample space, favourable events, computing probabilities
    • Dice: single and double die problems
    • Cards: standard 52-card deck problems (suits, face cards, numbered cards)
    • Balls in bags / coloured marbles
    • Days of week, months of year problems

Common Mistakes Students Make

Chapter-wise errors that cost marks in boards — avoid these
Real Numbers
✗
Writing "assume √2 = p/q where p and q are integers" without stating they are co-prime — this step is critical for the proof
✗
Not showing the contradiction explicitly — just saying "so it's irrational" without proving p and q share a common factor
✗
Confusing HCF × LCM = product of numbers (only valid for exactly TWO numbers, not three or more)
Polynomials
✗
Confusing "zeros of polynomial" with "roots of equation" — they're the same concept but framing matters in proofs
✗
Writing Sum of zeros = b/a instead of −b/a (missing the negative sign)
✗
Not verifying the relationship after finding zeros — losing easy verification marks
Linear Equations
✗
Making sign errors during elimination — especially when subtracting equations
✗
Not checking the solution by substituting back into both original equations
✗
In word problems: setting up the wrong variable — not reading the problem carefully enough
✗
Applying conditions a₁/a₂, b₁/b₂, c₁/c₂ without bringing equations to standard form ax+by+c=0 first
Quadratic Equations
✗
Middle-term splitting errors: not finding factors correctly or splitting with wrong signs
✗
Forgetting to write the condition a ≠ 0 when identifying a, b, c from the equation
✗
Computing discriminant incorrectly: D = b² − 4ac, not −b² − 4ac or b² + 4ac
✗
Rejecting negative roots in real-life problems without checking if they make contextual sense
Arithmetic Progressions
✗
Using Sₙ formula when nth term (aₙ) is required, or vice versa — read the question carefully
✗
Forgetting that d can be negative (decreasing AP) — don't assume d is positive
✗
Getting n as a fraction or negative and not recognising this means incorrect setup
✗
Mixing up aₙ (last term) and Sₙ (sum) — the question often uses both in the same problem
Triangles & Circles (Geometry)
✗
Not drawing an accurate diagram before starting geometry proofs — always draw and label clearly
✗
Skipping construction steps in BPT proof — every step of the proof must be explicitly stated
✗
Writing "AA similarity" without specifying WHICH angles are equal and WHY
✗
In circles: assuming OT ⊥ PT without stating theorem name and proving it first when required
✗
Confusing "equal tangents" theorem with "tangent perpendicular to radius" — different theorems, different proofs
Trigonometry
✗
Confusing sin²A with (sinA)² — they are the same, but writing sin²A = sinA² is wrong notation
✗
Forgetting to prove LHS = RHS independently — never cross-multiply or assume equality in identity proofs
✗
In heights and distances: not identifying which angle is elevation and which is depression
✗
Using wrong trigonometric ratio — always check which sides (opposite, adjacent, hypotenuse) you have
✗
Writing tan 90° = some number — it is undefined, not a large number
Mensuration
✗
Using diameter instead of radius in formulas — always halve the given diameter
✗
Adding the area of the joined face when computing surface area of combinations — it should be SUBTRACTED (hidden face)
✗
Not using l = √(r²+h²) for slant height of cone — forgetting this step
✗
In segment problems: using wrong triangle formula — area of equilateral triangle ≠ (1/2)base×height when not computed correctly
Statistics & Probability
✗
In Mean: using class boundaries instead of class marks (midpoints) as xᵢ — always use midpoint
✗
In Median: not identifying the correct median class (n/2, not n/2 + 1) — use cumulative frequency carefully
✗
In Mode: confusing f₀ and f₂ — f₀ is frequency of class BEFORE modal class, f₂ is AFTER
✗
In Probability: not listing all outcomes in sample space before computing — leads to wrong denominators
✗
Confusing face cards (12) with cards with faces: Ace is not a face card

Syllabus Changes for 2026–27

Aligned with NEP 2020 and NCF–SE 2023 — note all additions, removals, and modifications
Status Chapter / Topic Details
Removed Triangles — Pythagoras Theorem Proof and converse of Pythagoras Theorem removed. Was previously a major 5-mark proof question. No longer prescribed.
Removed Triangles — Areas of Similar Triangles Theorem: ratio of areas of similar triangles = square of ratio of corresponding sides — removed from syllabus.
Removed Constructions (entire chapter) Division of a line segment, construction of tangents to a circle — entire chapter removed. This was previously worth marks under geometry/constructions.
Removed Coordinate Geometry — Area of Triangle Formula for area of triangle using coordinates removed. Only Distance formula and Section formula (internal) retained.
Removed Real Numbers — Decimal Expansions Terminating and non-terminating recurring decimals link to rational numbers no longer explicitly listed (may still be tested implicitly).
Removed Pair of Linear Equations — Cross Multiplication Method Cross multiplication as a third algebraic method has been dropped. Only substitution and elimination are prescribed.
Modified Trigonometric Identities Emphasis on "only simple identities" — complex multi-step identity proofs are less likely. Focus on the three Pythagorean identities and their basic applications.
Modified Mensuration — Areas Related to Circles Segment problems now explicitly restricted to central angles of 60°, 90°, and 120° only. Other angles will not appear.
Modified Overall Curriculum Philosophy Shift from rote learning to competency-based education. More emphasis on real-life application, modelling, and contextualised problems. Increased focus on proof-writing for specified theorems.
Added/Emphasised Indian Knowledge System Integration Curriculum now explicitly integrates contributions of Indian mathematicians (e.g., Shridharacharya's formula, Aryabhata, Brahmagupta context). Questions may reference historical context.
Added/Emphasised Competency-based Learning New typology of questions: 22% marks for Analysing/Evaluating/Creating (higher-order thinking). Case-study based questions now carry significant weight.
Critical Alert for 2026-27 Students Do NOT prepare Pythagoras Theorem proof, Constructions chapter, Area of Triangle formula (coordinate geometry), or Cross Multiplication method for the board exam. These are officially removed from the prescribed syllabus. Spending time on them is counterproductive.

Final Strategy for 95%+ in Boards Master Algebra (20 marks) first — it is 25% of the paper. Then Geometry (15 marks) with both theorem proofs perfectly memorised. Then Trigonometry (12 marks) — the identity proofs and heights-and-distances. Do not neglect Statistics (grouped data formulas must be applied without errors). For Internal Assessment (20 marks), complete all lab activities, maintain a neat portfolio, and prepare thoroughly for Pen Paper Tests — these 20 marks can be secured almost completely with regular effort.
